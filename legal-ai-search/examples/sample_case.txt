Brown v. Anderson Construction Co.
456 F.3d 789 (9th Cir. 2022)

OVERVIEW:
This case involves a dispute over a construction contract where the contractor, Anderson Construction Co., allegedly failed to complete work according to specifications outlined in the agreement with property owner Sarah Brown.

FACTS:
On March 1, 2021, Sarah Brown entered into a written contract with Anderson Construction Co. for the construction of a residential addition. The contract price was $250,000 with completion scheduled for September 1, 2021. 

The contract specified:
- Construction of a 1,200 square foot addition
- Use of specified materials including hardwood flooring and granite countertops
- Completion within six months
- Final payment due upon satisfactory completion and inspection

Anderson Construction began work in March 2021 but encountered delays. By September 1, 2021, the project was only 60% complete. Additionally, the plaintiff alleged that inferior materials were substituted without permission, including laminate flooring instead of hardwood.

PROCEDURAL HISTORY:
Brown filed suit in state court alleging breach of contract and seeking damages. Anderson Construction removed the case to federal court based on diversity jurisdiction. The district court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff on the breach of contract claim.

ISSUES:
1. Whether Anderson Construction materially breached the contract by failing to complete work on time
2. Whether substitution of materials constituted breach
3. What damages are appropriate

HOLDING:
The Court of Appeals affirms the district court's finding of material breach. Anderson Construction breached the contract in multiple ways: (1) failure to complete work within the specified timeframe; (2) unauthorized substitution of inferior materials; and (3) deviation from agreed-upon specifications.

ANALYSIS:
Under basic contract law principles, a material breach occurs when a party fails to perform a substantial part of the contract or performs so defectively that the purpose of the contract is substantially defeated.

Time of Performance:
While time is not always of the essence in construction contracts, the six-month deadline was explicitly stated and was material to the plaintiff who needed to relocate for work. The substantial delay of over four months, without justification, constitutes material breach.

Material Substitution:
The unauthorized substitution of laminate for hardwood flooring was not a minor deviation. The contract specifically called for hardwood, and the substitution affected both the value and aesthetic of the project. This constitutes a separate material breach.

The Court applies the Restatement (Second) of Contracts § 241 factors for determining materiality of breach:
(a) Extent to which injured party deprived of expected benefit
(b) Adequacy of compensation in damages
(c) Extent of forfeiture by breaching party
(d) Likelihood of cure
(e) Conformity with standards of good faith and fair dealing

All factors weigh in favor of finding material breach.

DAMAGES:
The plaintiff is entitled to:
1. Cost of completion by another contractor ($180,000)
2. Diminution in value from material substitutions ($35,000)
3. Consequential damages from delayed occupancy ($12,000)
4. Reasonable attorney fees as provided in the contract

Total damages: $227,000

CONCLUSION:
Judgment affirmed. Anderson Construction Co. materially breached the contract through delay, unauthorized substitution of materials, and failure to meet specifications. Damages appropriately compensate the plaintiff for cost of completion and consequential losses.

The court emphasizes that contractors must strictly adhere to contract specifications, particularly regarding materials and timelines. Substitutions, even if similar in quality, require prior written approval.

CONCURRING OPINION:
Judge Martinez concurs but writes separately to note that modern construction contracts should include clearer provisions regarding acceptable material substitutions and force majeure events that might justify delays.

This case illustrates the importance of:
- Clear contract terms
- Strict adherence to specifications
- Written approval for any changes
- Timely performance
- Good faith dealing between parties

Keywords: breach of contract, construction law, material breach, damages, specific performance, substitution of materials, time of performance, consequential damages
